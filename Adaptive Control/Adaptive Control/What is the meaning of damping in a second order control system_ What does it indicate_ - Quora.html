When someone asks you what the damping ratio for a 2nd order system is, it is usually not a trick question!

If all the stars in the universe align in a manner that your plant is a well behaved, second order system (which it usually is not), it's exactly what it sounds like. A second order system can be interpreted as an oscillating mass with the natural frequency ωnωn and the damping ratio ζζ fixing the whole transfer function for us. And engineers love this fact because oscillation is one of the most studied modes of motion and we can predict a lot about the body's behaviour given only a few parameters.

The damping ratio decides whether the mass would be flung beyond imagination at resonance, or would sustain an input near the resonance frequency.

A big/small damping ratio decides the body's behaviour at input frequencies in the the vicinity of the natural frequency. I have quickly sketched a Bode magnitude plot of 3 transfer functions below; can you figure out which one has the highest damping ratio?

Written 31 Jan 2016

Damping refers to dissipation of energy in a system.

There are many ways of defining the order a system. It is the order of the differential equation characterizing the system. It is also the number of energy storage elements.

A RLC circuit is a second order system with the two energy storage elements being inductor and capacitor. Remember resistor can't store energy, it dissipates the energy.

Now, the presence of resistor introduces damping. Alternatively, in mechanical vibrations friction as act as damping. Remove the friction the vibrations go on for ever which is what we call an undamped system. In an LC circuit the absence of resistor which is the energy dissipating element makes the currents and voltage not decay to zero. This is the characteristic of undamped system.
